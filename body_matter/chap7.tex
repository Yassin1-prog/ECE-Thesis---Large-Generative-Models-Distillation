\chapter{Experiments and Results}
\label{chap:experiments}

This chapter presents the empirical evaluation of the distillation framework
described in Chapter~\ref{chap:framework}. The overarching objective of the
experimental campaign is to validate the central claim of the thesis: that,
for the same student model architecture and inference cost, distillation
training produces a strictly superior generative model compared to training
the student from scratch. The chapter is structured as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:setup} specifies the datasets, model configurations, training
protocol, computational resources, and evaluation methodology.
Section~\ref{sec:main_results} reports the main quantitative results for both
experimental scales. Sections~\ref{sec:abl_vitkd}, \ref{sec:abl_attn},
and~\ref{sec:abl_ws} present systematic ablation studies over the three
constituent components of the framework. Section~\ref{sec:synergy} analyses
component synergy and training dynamics. Section~\ref{sec:qualitative}
provides qualitative evidence through image and attention map visualisations.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the results and acknowledges
limitations.

% ============================================================
\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Datasets and Preprocessing}
\label{subsec:datasets}

Two benchmark datasets are employed across the two experimental scales. For
the small-scale setting, \textbf{CIFAR-10}~\cite{krizhevsky2009cifar} is
used, comprising 50{,}000 training images evenly distributed across 10 object
categories at a native resolution of $32 \times 32$ pixels. For the
large-scale setting, \textbf{ImageNet-1K}~\cite{russakovsky2015imagenet} is
employed, consisting of approximately 1.28 million training images spanning
1{,}000 semantic categories at variable resolutions, resized to $256 \times
256$ pixels for all experiments. In both cases, images are preprocessed
identically: a centre-crop is applied using the implementation of
\cite{dhariwal2021diffusion} to preserve the principal content of each image,
followed by random horizontal flipping as the sole data augmentation strategy.
Pixel values are normalised from the range $[0, 255]$ to $[-1, 1]$ prior to
being passed to the model, and class labels are used as conditioning
information throughout.

Two experimental pairs are defined. In the \emph{small-scale setting}, a
JiT-T/2 student (16.5M parameters) is distilled from a pretrained JiT-S/2

JiT-B/16 teacher on ImageNet-1K at $256 \times 256$ resolution. In both cases,
the teacher model is held fully frozen throughout distillation training, and
its exponential moving average (EMA) weights (\texttt{model\_ema1}) are used
as the reference checkpoint. The primary baseline in all comparisons is the
corresponding student architecture trained from scratch under an identical
training budget and optimisation configuration, with no access to teacher
supervision.

\label{subsec:training}

$\eta = \eta_\text{base} \times B_\text{eff} / 256$, with a base learning

evaluation time uses the first EMA track exclusively. Training is performed

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
\label{subsec:eval}

Generative quality is assessed using three standard metrics: the
Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)~\cite{heusel2017gans}, the Inception Score
(IS)~\cite{salimans2016improved}, and the Kernel Inception Distance
(KID)~\cite{binkowski2018demystifying}. FID and IS are computed over 50{,}000
generated images to ensure statistical reliability; KID is additionally
computed where a real-image reference directory is available, as it provides
an unbiased estimator of distributional discrepancy at finite sample sizes.
All metrics are evaluated using the \texttt{torch-fidelity} library. At
inference time, sampling is performed with the Heun second-order ODE solver
with 50 function evaluations (NFE), and classifier-free
guidance~\cite{ho2022classifier} is applied with a fixed scale and interval
per setting. All evaluations use the first EMA track of the student model.

\section{Ablation Study I: ViTKD Components}
\label{sec:abl_vitkd}
% ============================================================

The ViTKD loss $\mathcal{L}_\text{ViTKD}$ comprises two complementary terms:
a shallow-layer mimicking loss $\mathcal{L}_\text{mimic}$, which supervises
the student's early-block hidden states, and a deep-layer masked generation
loss $\mathcal{L}_\text{gen}$, which encourages the student's final-block
representations to carry sufficient information to reconstruct masked teacher
features, as detailed in Section~\ref{subsec:vitkd} of the framework chapter.
The following ablations isolate the contribution of each term and examine the
effect of timestep-aware loss reweighting. Unless otherwise stated, all
ablations in this section are conducted in the CIFAR-10 small-scale setting.

\subsection{Mimicking-Only vs.\ Full ViTKD}
\label{subsec:abl_mimic_vs_full}

To quantify the independent contribution of $\mathcal{L}_\text{gen}$, a
distilled student is trained using only the shallow mimicking objective
(enabled via \texttt{--mimic\_only}), in which the deep convolutional
projector and the learnable mask token $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ are absent. This
configuration is compared against the full ViTKD objective in
Table~\ref{tab:abl_mimic}. The generation loss is hypothesised to provide a
complementary supervisory signal that promotes richer deep representations,
beyond what direct feature regression at early layers can achieve.

\subsection{Effect of Min-SNR-$\gamma$ Weighting}
\label{subsec:abl_snr}

The flow-matching objective assigns equal weight to all diffusion timesteps by
default. The Min-SNR-$\gamma$ scheme~\cite{hang2023efficient} modulates this
by computing per-sample weights based on the signal-to-noise ratio of the
noisy input:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{SNR}(t) = \left(\frac{t}{1-t}\right)^2, \qquad
    w(t) = \frac{\min\!\bigl(\mathrm{SNR}(t),\, \gamma\bigr)}{\gamma},
    \label{eq:snr_weight}
\end{equation}
which down-weights samples at low-$t$ (high-noise) timesteps where the
denoising signal is weak and the distillation gradient is dominated by noise.
Four values are evaluated: $\gamma \in \{0, 0.25, 0.5, 1.0\}$, where $\gamma
= 0$ recovers the uniform baseline. Results are reported in
Table~\ref{tab:abl_snr}; Figure~\ref{fig:snr_curves} additionally illustrates
the mean distillation loss magnitude across discretised timestep bins for
representative values of $\gamma$.

\subsection{Symmetric vs.\ Asymmetric KL Divergence}
\label{subsec:abl_sym_kl}

By default, the attention loss uses the symmetric divergence
$\frac{1}{2}[D_\mathrm{KL}(\bar{\mathbf{A}}_T \| \bar{\mathbf{A}}_S) +
D_\mathrm{KL}(\bar{\mathbf{A}}_S \| \bar{\mathbf{A}}_T)]$, penalising
deviations in both directions. The asymmetric variant
(\texttt{--attn\_kd\_asymmetric}) retains only
$D_\mathrm{KL}(\bar{\mathbf{A}}_S \| \bar{\mathbf{A}}_T)$, which pushes
the student toward the teacher but exerts no regularisation on teacher
entropy. Table~\ref{tab:abl_sym} compares the two formulations.

\subsection{Timestep Gate Threshold $t_{\min}$}
\label{subsec:abl_tmin}

At timesteps close to zero, the noisy image $\mathbf{z} = t\mathbf{x} +
(1-t)\mathbf{e}$ is dominated by Gaussian noise, and the resulting attention
maps are near-uniform, contributing only gradient noise to the distillation
signal. A hard gate excludes samples with $t \leq t_{\min}$ from the
attention loss. Figure~\ref{fig:attn_entropy} motivates this design by
illustrating the empirically observed attention entropy as a function of
timestep. Table~\ref{tab:abl_tmin} reports FID for $t_{\min} \in \{0.0,
0.05, 0.1, 0.2\}$.

\section{Ablation Study III: Weight Selection Initialisation}

Weight selection~\cite{xu2024initializing} provides a structured, one-time
initialisation of the student parameters from a uniform subset of the
teacher's pretrained weights, prior to any gradient update. The following
two subsections disentangle its contribution from the online distillation
losses.

\subsection{Random Initialisation vs.\ Teacher-Seeded Initialisation}
\label{subsec:abl_ws_init}

The full distillation objective (ViTKD + attention KD) is applied in both
conditions, with the sole difference being whether the student begins from
random weights or from teacher-derived weights obtained via weight selection.
Final metric values are reported in Table~\ref{tab:abl_ws}, and FID
convergence curves over training epochs are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:ws_convergence}. Teacher-seeded initialisation is
hypothesised to accelerate convergence by placing the student in a region of
the loss landscape that is already partially aligned with the teacher's
representations, thereby reducing the initial distance that the online
distillation losses must bridge.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Interpreting the Results}
\label{subsec:disc_interp}

The experimental results collectively support the view that knowledge
distillation provides a form of structured regularisation during student
training, guiding the student's internal representations toward solutions
that generalise more effectively to the target data distribution. A key
enabler of this effect is JiT's pixel-space operation: because intermediate
hidden states correspond directly to noisy image patches rather than abstract
latent codes~\cite{rombach2022ldm}, the feature-level supervision imposed by
$\mathcal{L}_\text{mimic}$ and $\mathcal{L}_\text{gen}$ carries a
geometrically interpretable signal. Similarly, the attention maps exploited by
the attention KD component encode direct spatial relationships between image
regions, making teacher-to-student alignment a well-posed objective. The
weight selection initialisation can be understood as compressing the early
phase of distillation into a one-time pre-processing step: rather than
spending the initial training epochs reducing the distance between student and
teacher representations through gradient descent alone, the student begins in
an already-aligned configuration, and the online losses act as a refinement
mechanism thereafter.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{subsec:disc_limits}

Several limitations of the present framework merit acknowledgement. The
distillation loss scales $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $\gamma$ were kept at their
default values across all experiments; a systematic hyperparameter search
might yield further improvements at the cost of additional computation. The
attention timestep gate is a hard threshold, and the choice of $t_{\min}$
may be suboptimal for datasets or noise schedules that differ substantially
from those considered here. Furthermore, all experiments involve a fixed
teacher-to-student capacity ratio; the sensitivity of the framework to this
ratio, and in particular to very large capacity gaps, remains to be
characterised. Finally, although distillation incurs no additional cost at
inference time — the teacher and all distillation modules are discarded after
training — the joint forward pass through both teacher and student during
training increases per-epoch memory and compute requirements relative to
training the student from scratch.
